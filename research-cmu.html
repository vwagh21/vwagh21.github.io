Research

At CMU I work on one main line: hip exoskeleton assistance that listens to what you say and looks at what you see through glasses, then updates control on real hardware inside a few seconds. Before that, at UBC, I used off-the-shelf pose estimation to study upper-limb movement after stroke at a scale that would be painful with traditional mocap alone.

What the system is trying to do

“Can you help me a little more when I step forward?”

Rough sketch: glasses record audio and a fisheye frame. A laptop runs Whisper, preprocesses the image, runs the VLM with a prompt that privileges speech for which knob to move and vision for how big a step in context (incline, clutter, indoor vs outdoor). The model emits JSON the controller can parse; values slew smoothly on the device so torque does not jump. Gait-phase-aligned updates are on the wishlist, not the baseline yet.

Why not stop at manual tuning or HILO

Classic tuning and human-in-the-loop optimization often chase metabolic cost or expert-picked setpoints. They underweight comfort, language, and what the hallway actually looks like. I care about a loop where intent and environment both show up in the same update, in time for the next few steps, not after the session is over.

What is actually new here

  • VLMs on the exo problem Using a vision-language model to pick and nudge hip assist parameters, not just to chat about them.
  • Aria egocentric context Fisheye egocentric video plus mic from Meta Aria glasses as the primary wearable sensing path (gaze is a plausible extension later).
  • Real-time personalization Closing the loop from speech to parameter file on the order of a few seconds in measured pilot timing, not offline batch relabeling.
  • Shared autonomy framing User language steers the controller; vision backs magnitude and scene; the robot still respects actuator limits and smooth slews.

Controller knobs, schema, and what we measure

The hip controller uses a spline to shape assist torque. The two levers I talk about most are assistance magnitude (scale) and phase delay (delay) relative to the gait cycle. Phrases like “help me more” push scale; “it feels too late” pushes delay the other way. The VLM is prompted to output strict JSON the controller can parse; invalid text is a first-class failure mode. 

{
  "scale": +2.0,
  "delay": -0.03
}

Field names in deployment match whatever the device stack expects; the idea is the same: small structured deltas, not prose to the motor.

Pilot / system checks

  • Correct parameter (scale vs delay)
  • Direction (increase vs decrease)
  • Magnitude language (“a little” vs “a lot”)
  • Valid JSON, parseable end to end
  • Wall-clock latency budget

Human side

  • System Usability Scale (SUS)
  • Likert comfort, satisfaction, perceived control
  • NASA-TLX workload
  • Open-ended debriefs

The pilot label set is small but deliberate: 25 multimodal samples (fisheye frame + utterance), including five out-of-distribution phrases, with labels for which parameter should move and in which direction (magnitude left underspecified on purpose because it is user-specific).

Hypotheses and open questions

Hypotheses. (1) A VLM can turn free-form feedback into usable parameter edits. (2) Vision improves magnitude judgments, not just pretty captions. (3) Multimodal control feels better on comfort and perceived-control scores than speech alone in our protocol.

Questions I still poke at. How robust is this under vague phrases like “make it feel more natural”? How much does scene context actually move the numbers vs. overfitting to texture? What step size can people even perceive? Does this framing beat static tuning on the scales we care about?

Failure modes and next builds

Observed or expected breaks

  • Model returns prose instead of JSON
  • Wrong parameter or wrong sign
  • Vision overweighted vs. clear audio intent
  • Ambiguous instructions with no safe default

Roadmap items

  • Gait-phase synchronized updates
  • Gaze as another signal
  • Obstacle and terrain hooks
  • Preference models + history (bandits / session learning)

Parallel CMU track: how little post-stroke data do you need to predict joint moments during gait? I align messy IMU stacks in OpenSim with MATLAB glue, simulate consistent sensor streams from mocap-driven models, and train temporal convnets in PyTorch. Pretrain on large able-bodied corpora, finetune with well under one percent of subject-specific labels in our setting, still get a large jump in prediction quality.
