Research

At CMU, the focus is one thing: hip exoskeleton assistance that responds to what the wearer sees and says, on real hardware, within real latency budgets. Before that, at UBC, off-the-shelf pose estimation was used to study upper-limb movement after stroke — at a scale that would be painful with traditional mocap alone.

Human-in-the-loop adaptation for hip exoskeleton control

MetaMobility Lab, Carnegie Mellon University · Advised by Dr. Inseung Kang

People adjust how much assist they want all the time. That should be normal, not a lab visit. The work connects rough speech and egocentric video to actual controller changes — without requiring clean labels per person, and without making the wearer wait.

Problem framing

The hip controller exposes a handful of timing and gain knobs. They matter a lot for how the device feels. Hand tuning doesn't scale. Pure imitation from demos misses the harder part: preferences shift mid-walk. The full loop matters — reading multimodal signals, proposing updates that respect how the controller actually works, and staying honest about model uncertainty and vague feedback.

Multimodal interface and edge stack

Pipelines start on the body: smart-glasses video and whatever the person said into the mic. Audio gets transcribed, then the frame, transcript, and a short snapshot of recent parameters go into a vision-language model — so it's not guessing in a vacuum. Outputs are mapped to structured assist settings for the biotorque-style hip controller. A lot of the work is wiring record, inference, and push-to-device to behave on something Jetson-sized, with quantization and latency that don't leave the wearer hanging.

Contextual bandits handle the cases where one sentence isn't enough to pick a good assist regime. The goal is slow improvement across sessions, without treating every label as ground truth.

Personalized torque estimation (locomotion)

Figure torque / ASB

A separate CMU thread, same big picture: how little post-stroke data do you need to predict joint moments during gait? Messy IMU setups are aligned using OpenSim and MATLAB, consistent sensor streams are simulated from mocap-driven models, and temporal convnets are trained in PyTorch. Pretraining on large able-bodied runs and finetuning with a small per-subject slice yielded about a 27% accuracy improvement — with well under one percent of subject-specific training data.

Earlier research at UBC

Several years in the Neuroplasticity, Imagery and Motor Behaviour Lab, asking a straightforward question: can cheap cameras and MediaPipe do real kinematics work for stroke, beyond just demos?

OpenCV and MediaPipe on a gamified reaching task — many trials and frames, then statistics tying kinematics to compensation patterns that matter in rehab. Published in Journal of Neuroengineering and Rehabilitation (2025). DOI
