At CMU: hip exoskeleton control that reacts to speech and what you see through glasses. Before that, at UBC: MediaPipe and stroke kinematics at a scale mocap struggles to match.

Thesis · MetaMobility Lab

Vision-language models for real-time personalized hip exoskeleton control

MetaMobility Lab, Carnegie Mellon University · Advised by Dr. Inseung Kang

The idea is simple: what you say and what you see should be able to nudge assist on the fly, without a lab visit every time it feels wrong. I use a VLM on transcribed speech plus egocentric frames, and map that to structured updates the hip controller can actually use.

Example

“Can you help me a little more when I step forward?”

Speech carries intent (more assist, different timing). The egocentric view nudges how big a change makes sense for the scene. The model answers in JSON the controller can parse; the device smooths updates so torque does not lurch.

Motivation

Manual tuning and a lot of HILO work optimize numbers experts care about and leave out language, comfort, and the hallway you are actually in. I want intent and context in the same loop the wearer can steer.

What this pushes

  • VLMs tied to real assist parameters, not just chat.
  • Egocentric glasses (Meta Aria) as the main wearable sensing path.
  • Shared autonomy: user language steers, vision backs scene-scale judgment, hardware limits still matter.
  • Session-level learning when one sentence is not enough (e.g. contextual bandits), without pretending every label is clean.

Controller interface

The hip spline is steered mainly by assistance magnitude and phase delay relative to gait. The VLM is pushed toward strict JSON so the stack does not have to guess free text. 

{
  "scale": +2.0,
  "delay": -0.03
}

Limits and next steps

Still wrestling with prose instead of JSON, wrong knob or sign, vision talking over clear audio, and vague phrases with no safe default. Next up: gait-phase-aware updates, richer preference memory, gaze if it actually helps.
