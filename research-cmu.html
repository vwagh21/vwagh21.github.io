Research

At CMU I mostly work on one thing: hip exoskeleton assistance that responds to what the wearer sees and says, on real hardware, with real latency budgets. Before that, at UBC, I used off-the-shelf pose estimation to study upper-limb movement after stroke at a scale that would be painful with traditional mocap alone.

Human-in-the-loop adaptation for hip exoskeleton control

MetaMobility Lab, Carnegie Mellon University · Advised by Dr. Inseung Kang

People tweak how much assist they want all the time. I'd like that to be normal, not a lab visit. So I build ways to go from rough speech plus egocentric video to actual controller changes, without pretending we have tons of clean labels per person, and without the robot taking forever to answer.

Problem framing

Our hip controller exposes a handful of timing and gain knobs. They matter a lot for how the device feels. Hand tuning does not scale. Pure imitation from demos also misses the messy part: what you want can change mid-walk. I care about the full loop: read multimodal signals, propose updates that respect how the controller actually works, and stay honest about model error and vague user feedback.

Multimodal interface and edge stack

The pipelines I run start on the body: smart-glasses video plus whatever the person said into the mic. That's a different starting point than a mocap suite. I transcribe audio with a small speech stack, then feed the frame, transcript, and a short snapshot of recent parameters into a vision-language model so it's not guessing in a vacuum. Outputs get squeezed into structured assist settings that match our biotorque-style hip controller. A lot of the grunt work is wiring record, inference, and push-to-device so it behaves on something Jetson-sized, with quantization and latency that don't leave the wearer hanging.

I also use contextual bandits when one sentence is not enough to pick a good assist regime. The goal is slow improvement across sessions without treating every label as perfect.

Personalized torque estimation (locomotion)

Figure torque / ASB

Separate CMU thread, same big picture: how little post-stroke data do you need to predict joint moments during gait? I line up messy IMU setups using OpenSim plus MATLAB scripts, simulate consistent sensor streams from mocap-driven models, then train temporal convnets in PyTorch. Pretraining on large able-bodied runs and finetuning with a tiny per-subject slice gave us about a 27% accuracy bump in our setting, with well under one percent of subject-specific training data.

Earlier research at UBC

I spent several years in the Neuroplasticity, Imagery and Motor Behaviour Lab asking a blunt question: can cheap cameras plus MediaPipe do real kinematics work for stroke, not just demos?

Post-stroke reaching (MediaPipe)

OpenCV and MediaPipe on a gamified reaching task. Lots of trials and frames, then statistics tying kinematics to compensation patterns you care about in rehab. Paper is in Journal of Neuroengineering and Rehabilitation (2025). DOI
